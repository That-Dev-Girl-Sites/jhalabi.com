Trust is a critical ingredient to the success of any organization. You trust your colleagues to make rational decisions regarding how to get the work done. Your colleagues trust you to do the same. 

Trust goes hand-in-hand with mutual respect. You cannot trust someone if you do not respect them, and you cannot respect someone without trust. They are practically synonyms.

The loss of that trust is a big deal. 

As a personal example: A department outside of mine is undergoing a major organizational change, in which the institutional knowledge for a particular service will be lost in a matter of months. I rely on this particular service as part of my core job, so for me (and others like me in my organization), this is a big deal.

However, when I asked department leaders about a plan for a continuation of support for that service, they did not respond with a plan. In fact, they did not answer me at all. Instead, they complained to other people about the fact that I asked the question. 

This is leadership failure in several ways. This situation demonstrates:

1. A lack of respect by bringing extraneous people into the conversation. The extra people are unable to answer my question, which makes their involvement inefficient and irrational.
1. A lack of respect in not replying to the question at all. While I am a firm believer of "no answer is still an answer," it is unhelpful in this case. The lack of any answer forces the conclusion that there is no plan, but that is merely an assumption. Assumptions are not actionable.
1. A lack of real leadership. A leader leads by example. Modeling a behavior of both avoiding questions and talking behind someone's back indicates to others that this is acceptable behavior.

Leadership failures break trust.
